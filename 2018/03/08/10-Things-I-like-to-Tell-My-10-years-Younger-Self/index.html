10 Things I like to Tell My 10-years Younger Self

Mar 08, 2018

After 10 years of graduation, here is my list of lessons I learnt over the past 10 years.

  1. Basics doesn’t mean easy
  2. Hard Working Pays Off
  3. Connections, Connections, Connections, and EQ
  4. Invest in yourself
  5. Happiness is in The Goalden Mean
  6. That other dimension called Behind Closed Doors
  7. False positive ≠ False negative
  8. Focus
  9. The other side is always greener
  10. “Don’t be afraid of the new arenas.” — Elon Musk

1. Basics doesn’t mean easy

TL;DR Learn the basics by heart, and implement things from scratch

  • geeksforgeeks.org
  • Hackerrank.com
  • Interviewing.io

For machine learning and data science

  • Kaggle.com

To better predict the future, you need to look back at history (history “most of the” repeats itself)

And remember “Simple can be Harder than Complex.” ― Steve Jobs

2. Hard Working Pays Off

TL;DR I may not be that smart or talented the way I may look like, but I can confirm to you that at least 80-90% of my success is due to hard working rather than just being smart or talented.

First, just do whatever is asked from you as long as you are accumulating new knowledge and/or skills. Then, you will be able to pick what you want to do. (This lesson I learnt from celebrities.

Roughly, 80% of what I know now I learnt it after graduation not during my collage.

3. Connections, Connections, Connections, and EQ

TL;DR Your connections is as important or even more important than your technical skills. Also, be nice to Win Friends and Influence People

Hard working is powerful and effective. but being nice is what can get you to the next level.

Search for EQ
and How to Win Friends and Influence People by Dale Carnegie

In addition to the virtual world including LinkedIn, and Twitter. I found Conferences to be the my best place to meet people with similar interests and make new high-quality connections.

4. Invest in yourself

Change is the only constant and “Intelligence is the ability to adapt to change.” - Stephen Hawking

Invest in yourself, have it as a backup plan (or insurance)

Safaribooksonline.com

5. The Goalden Mean

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_mean_(philosophy)

Happiness is in balance

6. That other dimension called Behind Closed Doors

Strategic thinking and
Plan to keep yourself sane

7. False positive ≠ False negative

False positive is not equale to false negative

https://www.workrules.net

8. Focus

Horizontal vs Vertical knowledge.

First, I wanted web, then ML, while now I am working on Navigation Algorithms.

9. The other side is always greener

Only switch to the other side, if your current side is not green, at all. This is important because it aligns well with the previous point “Focus”.

If you are searching for challenges, you can always find new challenges at your current side. You don’t have to switch sides to find new one. If you are from a computer engineering background like me, you will agree with me that no switching is free. In fact, most of the time we spend we are tying to reduce if not to eliminate switching cost.

10. “Don’t be afraid of the new arenas.” — Elon Musk

Here comes my final point, and the closest point to my heart. Don’t be afraid of the new arenas. Computer Science is advancing in a very high speed pace, and if you feel afraid from new arenas, Computer science is definitely not for you. Honestly, for this specific reason, I feel myself I am celebrating my 10 years anniversary in this field, and I am looking towards spending my next 10 years in it too. In other words, I love competing in new arenas, and that is why computer science is the best fit for me.

In fact, if there is a single reason why I spent 10 years in software industry, this one would be it.
